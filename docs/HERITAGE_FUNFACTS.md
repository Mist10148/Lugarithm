# Heritage Fun Facts — Ambient Town Lore

**Purpose.** This document is the source material for the *town NPCs* the player
meets while walking each overworld town (top-down scene) before boarding the
jeepney. These are *ambient*, lighthearted facts and trivia — flavor for the
townsfolk to chat about.

**Boundary (important).** These NPC facts must **not** restate the payoffs that
`HERITAGE_RESEARCH.md` reserves for the in-jeepney **heritage reveal** (the
father's-journal / artifact moment with the main front-seat character). Those
reserved beats — the "why" behind the Oton gold mask, your grandmother's role
in Molo's cloth economy, the Tigbauan First Ambush, the Miag-ao St. Christopher
"carved in our own clothes" secret, the San Joaquin son-to-father church story —
stay with the main passenger. The town NPCs gesture *toward* the wonder of each
place but never spend the reveal. Think postcards, not the secret letter.

The content here maps to `TownNpcDialogueLibrary` (one small hub conversation per
NPC, 1–4 NPCs per level).

## Level 0 — Iloilo City (Tutorial: the garage district & Calle Real)

**Detailed lore.**
- Iloilo City grew rich on cloth and sugar in the 1800s; its old downtown spine,
  **Calle Real** (now J.M. Basa St.), is lined with art-deco and beaux-arts
  buildings from that boom — a heritage commercial street people still shop on.
- The city was nicknamed the **"Queen City of the South"** in the Spanish/American
  eras, a port that handled the province's sugar and textile trade.
- The **jeepney** itself is the everyday hero here — a postwar Filipino reinvention
  of surplus military jeeps into shared public transport, hand-decorated and proud.

**Fun facts / trivia.**
- Iloilo is a **UNESCO Creative City of Gastronomy** (2023) — the food is
  officially world-class, not just lola's opinion.
- The **Dinagyang Festival** every January fills the streets with drumming and
  painted dancers honoring the Santo Niño; it's one of the country's biggest.
- Locals call the gentle Ilonggo accent "malambing" — sing-songy and sweet;
  visitors swear everyone sounds like they're flirting.
- A proper Ilonggo merienda runs on **batchoy**, pancit, and ice-cold pop.

## Level 1 — Molo

**Detailed lore.**
- **Molo** was once its own town and a famous **Chinese-mestizo district**;
  its grand stone houses ("Molo mansions") were built by trading families.
- **Molo Church (St. Anne Parish)**, Gothic-Renaissance in coral stone, is widely
  called the **"women's church"** — its interior saints are all female figures.
- Molo plaza and its surrounding streets are a postcard of old-money Iloilo:
  arcaded houses, a bandstand, and the white church spires over the rooftops.

**Fun facts / trivia.**
- **Pancit Molo** — the cozy dumpling soup — is named after this very district.
  (It's "molo" the place, not "molo" the noodle: there are no noodles in it!)
- Sunday after Mass, the smell of fresh **pan de Molo** and cooking from the
  plaza kitchens drifts all the way down the street.
- The old houses are so photogenic that couples line up to take wedding photos
  on the church steps.

## Level 2 — Oton

**Detailed lore.**
- **Oton** sits on the coast just past Iloilo City and is one of the oldest
  settlements on the island — a **pre-colonial port** long before the Spanish.
- Near the old shipyards by the shore, archaeologists uncovered an astonishing
  **gold artifact** (a death mask) from a centuries-old burial — proof of how
  rich and connected this coast once was.
- Oton's people were known as fine craftsmen — boat-builders and smiths
  (**panday**) who worked wood and iron for the sea trade.

**Fun facts / trivia.**
- The town's heritage is celebrated each year with a festival where people don
  **gold face paint and paper** in honor of the famous mask.
- The **Batiano River** still threads through town the way it has for centuries.
- Oton is proud "older than it looks" — locals love reminding visitors that
  there was history sailing here before any church was built.

## Level 3 — Tigbauan

**Detailed lore.**
- **Tigbauan** is a quiet southern coastal town with a deep heritage in
  **hablon** — handloom weaving of cotton, piña, and jusi into plaids and stripes.
- Its church, **St. John of Sahagun**, is famous for an ornate, almost lace-like
  **churrigueresque facade** — unusually elaborate for a small town.
- Tigbauan also holds a quiet "first": it's remembered as the site of an early
  **Jesuit school** in the islands, centuries ago.

**Fun facts / trivia.**
- A weaving pattern is basically a **loop**: "three threads red, one white, repeat"
  — tell the loom the rule once and a whole blanket appears.
- You can sometimes read **where a person is from** by the pattern they wear —
  cloth carries a regional memory.
- The clack-clack of the looms is a sound the town says **hasn't stopped in
  two hundred years**.

## Level 4 — Miag-ao

**Detailed lore.**
- **Miag-ao Church (Santo Tomás de Villanueva)** is a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**,
  built in the late 1700s as a **fortress-church** — thick walls and watchtowers
  to shield the town from coastal raiders.
- Its honey-colored facade is carved in soft local **sandstone/coral**, weathered
  warm and golden over the centuries.
- Miag-ao keeps the weaving tradition alive too, through **Indag-an** hablon
  cooperatives, much like Tigbauan down the coast.

**Fun facts / trivia.**
- The facade famously features a giant **coconut tree** climbing up the center —
  the "tree of life," with everyday plants, birds, and local life carved around it.
- It's two-in-one architecture: a place to **pray in and hide in** — faith and
  defense in the same stone.
- Photographers chase the late-afternoon light here, when the whole front glows gold.

## Level 5 — San Joaquin

**Detailed lore.**
- **San Joaquin** is the southernmost town on the Iloilo coastal route, where the
  road meets long pebble-and-sand beaches facing the sea.
- The **San Joaquin Church** is unique in the Philippines for the dramatic
  **military relief carved across its facade** — soldiers and cavalry in battle,
  the only church front of its kind in the country.
- Up the hill stands the **Campo Santo**, a baroque Spanish-era **cemetery**
  declared a national cultural treasure, with old convent ruins, a kiln, and a
  round stone well beside it.

**Fun facts / trivia.**
- Almost everything in San Joaquin **faces the sea** — even the dead, in the
  hillside Campo Santo.
- The caretaker will tell you a cemetery isn't a sad place, just **"a place that
  remembers."**
- It's a favorite end-of-road stop: pebble beaches, sea breeze, and the quietest
  sunsets on the coast.

### Bonus interlude — Guimbal (drive-through)

- The little **Taytay Tigre** ("tiger bridge") is Spanish-built, with stone tigers
  guarding the way into town.
- Guimbal glows warm because it's built from yellow **igang** coral stone pulled
  from the shore and from Guimaras — the same warm stone you'll see up the road
  in Miag-ao. The whole coast shared its bones to build itself.
